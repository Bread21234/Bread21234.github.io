Decision Making: How Our Minds Create Bias and How to Handle It


William Clymire Allen Highschool Computer Science III David Ben-Yaakov December 2, 2022

Bias: The Unstoppable Force

Compare bias to infestation. In every decision a human makes, there is some type of bias. Whether said bias comes from logical reasoning or superstition varies, but one thing remains true; there is no such thing as an unbiased decision. While bias is neither good nor bad, it is important that each person recognizes how susceptible they are so that they may improve their own decision making. I am not exempt from this as I am also subject to extreme bias at times. In relation to the computer science ISP, I believe that although my group members and I will attempt to control our bias, we will subconsciously lean towards certain ideas more than others. Specifically, the biases that will cause the most impact in the project will be complexity, the illusion of control, confirmation, survivorship, and Ikea effect biases.

Complexity

Firstly, complexity bias will have a massive impact on myself and how the project commences. Complexity bias is what occurs when time pressure, large quantities of information, and other stressors cause an individual to perceive a task as more complex than it is, interfering with their decision making. There is a “...relationship of task stress to decision quality called the Yerkes-Dawson Law”(Arnott 7). For us, the best choice to avoid too much complexity bias is by creating and fitting to a set schedule. Much of complexity bias comes from stress, especially related to time pressure, so the easiest way to avoid it would be to build a descriptive gantt chart. This way, we can stay on time and not feel as overwhelmed by an approaching due date.

Control

In addition to complexity bias, the illusion of control bias will also play a big part in this project. The illusion of control bias is the human tendency to believe they have control over a situation which impacts their decision making. In the context of the ISP, our rigorous planning can introduce control bias. Because we make the schedule ourselves, we have the illusion of having more control over the assignment than we do. As a result, we may feel more relaxed, hindering our decision making. We can overcome this bias by acknowledging how much work we have to do and making sure we properly assess what we have control over and what we do not.

Confirmation

Personally, I believe confirmation bias will have one of the greatest impacts on our decision making than some of the other biases. Confirmation bias is a bias that occurs when “‘...decision makers seek confirmatory evidence and exclude search for discomforting information’”(Arnott 7). Essentially, people avoid displeasing information in favor of information that supports what they already believe. For the ISP, the most confirmation bias will occur when a new idea challenges the work we have already done and may require us to redo parts. In order to avoid having to remake parts of the project, confirmation bias will kick in and interfere with our decision making. Our best way of avoiding this bias is to have a project manager who is willing to make the tough decisions even when it means more work for the group.

Survivorship

Perhaps the most persistent and obscure bias affecting the project will be survivorship bias. Survivorship bias is what occurs when people pay “...too much attention to success, while glossing over failures”(Timely). As a result, people often become overly optimistic and underestimate what is left to be done. This could affect the ISP by making us focus too much on our small successes rather than looking at the bigger picture. It is easy to get carried away in the progress that we make to the point where we neglect what still needs to be fixed and/or completed. In order to counteract survivorship bias, we can “...always consider pessimistic viewpoints…” in order to gather the most accurate idea of our progress (Timely). Ikea Effect

Along with survivorship bias, another obscure bias that could have vastly detrimental effects is known as the Ikea effect. The Ikea effect is known as the effect which causes one to place more value on the work they have done themself, “...often while discounting other people’s smart ideas or good work”(Timely) In the context of the ISP, this bias is extremely dangerous as it can easily lead to infighting if we each value our own ideas more than those of our peers (Ikea Effect). In order to counter it, the best option is to seek a second opinion from another member in order to easily settle the dispute, both alleviating tension and increasing the effectiveness of decision making. Why Does it Matter?

No matter how hard we try, it is impossible to eradicate all bias. However, that does not mean we should simply let it control our decision making. Instead, it is important that we understand each of the biases that affect us so that we may act accordingly to fight against them. The computer science ISP is a long project that will require many decisions, so it is important that we take into consideration how biases such as complexity, the illusion of control, confirmation, survivorship, and the Ikea effect will affect each member of the group.
